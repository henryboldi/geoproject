"STAR SPANGLED BANNER"

                                                

                                               

   Most students of history know that Francis Scott Key and Colonel John S. Skinner were on board a  vessel in Baltimore's harbor on September'13-14, 1814, when the British commenced a bombardment of Fort Henry just off the shoreline.  Both men searched anxiously throughout the night for some sign of the American flag atop the Fort, but it was only by "the dawn's early light"  after the "rockets red glare and the bombs bursting in air" that Key was able to discern that the Flag still waved.  According to history he then penned "The Star Spangled Banner" on the back of a letter he had in his waistcoat pocket.  The tune he employed was "Anacreon in Heaven," a popular song in 1814.

The original "Star Spangled Banner" which Key so gratefully saw still flying over Fort McHenry is still preserved today.  It originally measured thirty by forty two feet.  (What was its area, perimeter and the length of its diagonal?)  A seamstress, Mary Young Pickersgill, had been commissioned to make the flag and was handsomely paid $450.90 for her services.  She utilized 400 yards of first quality hand-woven wool bunting for the project.  

Though the flag remained in the possession of the family of the Lieutenant Colonel who commanded Fort McHenry for  a century, in 1914 it was gifted to the Smithsonian Institute in Washington D.C.  It was in great need of preservation, and several women experienced in  needlework undertook the task.  In so doing they had to stitch a linen backing and reinforce stripe stitching.  The historic flag had to be cut down to twenty eight by thirty two feet. ( Is this still proportional to the original measurements?)

Today this "Star Spangled Banner" is still on permanent exhibition in the Smithsonian.  It is an emblem of honor, of sacrifice, of liberty and it evokes the same love of country today as it did when Francis Scott Key "so proudly hailed" that "our flag was still there."
