CONGRUENCE

 

The stars is the blue union are, of course, polygons, because they are many sided figures.  But they are not the typical regular concave polygons we have been dealing with in class.  Rather they are concave polygons, because some of the interior angles are greater than 180 degrees.  The stars on any one American flag are congruent, because they all have the same size and shape.

There is any interesting historical snippet about why the stars are shaped as they are, even from the earliest version of "Old Glory."  Supposedly  George Washington had envisioned a flag with stars containing six points.  Betsy Ross, however, was determined to have a 5 pointed star.  She argued that a five pointed star could be made easily, if one folded a piece of paper in the correct fashion.  A brief description of this process follows with pictures courtesy of:

according to their permission procedure.

Step 1. Fold an 8-1/2" x 10" piece of paper in half.

Step 2. Fold and unfold in half both ways to form creased center lines. (Note: be sure paper is still folded in half.)

Step 3. Bring corner (1) right to meet the center line. Be sure to fold from the vertical crease line.

Step 4. Bring corner (1) left till edges coincide, then make the fold.

 

Step 5. Bring corner (2) left and fold.

Step 6. Bring corner (2) right until edges coincide. Then fold.

Step 7. Cut on the angle as shown in the picture. Then unfold the small piece.

Step 8. Marvel at your perfect (we hope!) 5-pointed star! If your star is not perfect, take a fresh piece of paper (8-1/2" x 10" -- not 8-1/2" x 11") and return to Step 1.
